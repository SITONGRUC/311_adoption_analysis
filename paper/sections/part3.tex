\section{Hypothesis Test}

% \paragraph{Data and design.}
To test whether 311 adoption is associated with changes in city government spending,
adoption dates from Section~1 are combined with the Census Annual Survey of
Government Finances, Individual Unit Data, for 2017 through 2022
\citep{census_govfin2022}.
Total general expenditure per capita is constructed from the item-level fixed-width
files by summing current-operations (E-prefix), capital-construction (F-prefix), and
other-capital-outlay (G-prefix) items, plus general interest on debt (I89), while
excluding utility enterprise operations.
Cities are matched to the finance data directly via Census place FIPS code,
achieving a 100\,\% match for the 153-city regression sample (99 adopted with known
year plus 54 never-adopted cities).
The estimator is two-way fixed effects (city and year fixed effects), with standard
errors clustered at the city level.
The primary data constraint is panel length: Individual Unit Finance files are
publicly available only for 2017 through 2022, so cities that adopted before 2017
appear as always-treated throughout the panel with no observable pre-period.
The 148 cities with confirmed 311 but unknown adoption timing are excluded from
the regression because the post-treatment indicator requires a specific year.

% \paragraph{Baseline result.}
Table~\ref{tab:twfe} reports estimates from two specifications.
In the full sample (867 city-year observations), the coefficient on \texttt{post311}
is $-0.025$ (SE $= 0.034$, $p = 0.47$), indicating no statistically or economically
significant association between 311 adoption and log per capita total general
expenditure.
A sensitivity specification restricting to cities adopting after 2012 yields a
similar estimate of $-0.020$ (SE $= 0.035$, $p = 0.56$).
Both specifications are statistically insignificant; the high table $R^2$ reflects
variance absorbed by city and year fixed effects, not the treatment indicator.

% \paragraph{Event study.}
Figure~\ref{fig:eventstudy} presents event-study estimates for the subset of cities
with observable pre- and post-adoption windows in the 2017--2022 panel.
The treated group consists of 18 cities that adopted in 2019 through 2022, which are
the only cohorts with non-reference pre-period observations given the panel start
date.
The control group is the 54 never-adopted cities.
The event window is $[-3,\,+3]$ relative to the adoption year.
Pre-period coefficients at $k=-3$ and $k=-2$ are small and statistically
insignificant (0.047, SE\,$=$\,0.060; 0.012, SE\,$=$\,0.033), consistent with
parallel pre-trends.
Post-adoption coefficients are also insignificant throughout, though confidence
intervals widen as cohort support thins.
The bottom panel of the figure reports the number of treated cities contributing
to each event-time cell; the coefficient at $k=+3$ (three cities) should not be
interpreted.

% \paragraph{Interpretation and limitations.}
The null result is informative rather than the product of insufficient power alone.
311 is primarily an administrative routing and case-management system whose direct
operating costs are modest relative to total city expenditure.
Three limitations qualify the findings.
First, the panel covers 2017--2022 only; any fiscal adjustment predating 2017 is
undetectable.
Second, the event study uses 18 treated cities and is underpowered for detecting
moderate-sized effects.
Third, the regression sample skews toward larger, better-documented cities
(mean population 497k), which may differ from smaller jurisdictions in how 311
interacts with local fiscal management.
