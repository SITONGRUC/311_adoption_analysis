\section{Descriptive Analysis}

% \paragraph{Timing.}
Figure~\ref{fig:timing} shows annual and cumulative documented 311 adoptions for the 99 cities with verified launch years. Adoption was gradual rather than concentrated in a single wave: Baltimore adopted in 1996, Chicago and Houston followed in 1999, and uptake continued through the 2000s and 2010s. The figure has two limits. First, 148 additional cities are confirmed 311 adopters but lack verifiable launch years, so the chart understates total prevalence. Second, recent years may be overstated because 2026 web searches more easily recover current launch announcements, creating possible ascertainment bias.

% \paragraph{Geography.}
Figure~\ref{fig:region} plots within-region adoption shares across the 301 cities in
the main sample.
The Northeast has the highest share of cities with a documented formal-311 year
(52\,\% of 27 cities), while the West contributes 124 cities (mostly California)
but the lowest documented-year share (23\,\%).
California cities frequently operate 311-adjacent platforms under proprietary branding
without accessible launch records, placing the majority in the ``adopted, year
unknown'' category.
Across all four regions, the unknown-year segment is substantial, indicating that
the 99-city timing sample undercounts total 311 prevalence and skews toward cities
with stronger documentation practices.

% \paragraph{Adopter profile.}
Table~\ref{tab:summary} compares cities across adoption-status groups.
Cities with documented formal-311 adoption years are substantially larger (mean
population 497k) than both unknown-year adopters (176k) and non-adopters (236k).
This size gradient has two implications.
First, the timing analysis in Figure~\ref{fig:timing} reflects large-city diffusion
trajectories, not the full distribution of cities above 100{,}000 residents.
Second, the regression sample in Section~3 skews toward better-documented, larger
cities; any fiscal associations estimated there apply primarily to that subpopulation.
